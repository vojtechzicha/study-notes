Bilinear and Quadratic Forms

Quadratic form

Definition: \mathcal{Q}_{B} is a quadratic form, if it takes a vector and outputs a real number. \mathcal{Q}_{B}\mathcal{:V}\mathbb{\rightarrow R}.

\mathcal{Q}_{B}(v)\mathcal{= B}(v,v) = \mathcal{B}_{S}(v,v)

Example: \mathcal{Q:}\mathbb{R}^{3}\mathbb{\rightarrow R;}\mathcal{Q}(x) = x^{T}x = x_{1}^{2} + x_{2}^{2} + x_{3}^{2}, always positive or zero

Example: \mathcal{Q:}\mathbb{R}^{2}\mathbb{\rightarrow R;}\mathcal{Q}(x) = x^{T}Ax = a_{11}x_{1}^{2} + \left( a_{12} + a_{21} \right)x_{1}x_{2} + a_{22}x_{2}^{2}, contains zero

Example: \mathcal{Q:F \rightarrow}\mathbb{R;}\mathcal{Q}(f) = f^{2}(1)

\mathcal{Q}_{B}(\alpha x) = \alpha^{2}\mathcal{Q(}x), (prove by B(\alpha x,\alpha x))

Property: 1 \in R\left( \mathcal{Q} \right)\mathcal{\Rightarrow Q}(x) = 1;\mathcal{Q}(\alpha x) = \alpha^{2}\mathcal{Q}(x) = \alpha^{2} : if 1 is in the range, all positive numbers and zero is in the range; similarly if -1 is in the range, all negative numbers and zero is in the range.

Property: \mathcal{Q}(0)\mathcal{= Q}(0 \bullet 0) = 0 \bullet \mathcal{Q}(0) = 0

A bilinear form generating a quadratic form is not unique.
By adding any antisymmetric bilinear form, I’d get a new one.

Example: Find a symmetric bilinear form \mathcal{B}_{S} for quadratic form \mathcal{Q}_{B}.

\begin{array}{r} \mathcal{B}_{S}(x + y,x + y)\mathcal{= B}(x,x)\mathcal{+ B}(x,y)\mathcal{+ B}(y,x)\mathcal{+ B}(y,y) \\ \mathcal{Q}(x)\mathcal{= Q}(x) + 2\mathcal{B}_{S}(x,y)\mathcal{+ Q}(y) \\ \mathcal{B}_{S}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}\left( \mathcal{Q}(x + y)\mathcal{- Q}(x)\mathcal{- Q}(y) \right) \end{array}\

A symmetric bilinear form generating a quadratic is unique
and given by \mathcal{B}_{S}(x,y,) = \frac{1}{2}\left( \mathcal{Q}(x + y)\mathcal{- Q}(x)\mathcal{- Q}(y) \right).

Example: \mathcal{B}(x,y) = 2x_{1}y_{1} - 3x_{1}y_{2} + 5x_{2}y_{1} - x_{2}y_{2};\varepsilon = \{\lbrack 1,0\rbrack,\lbrack 0,1\rbrack\}

B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & - 3 \\ 5 & - 1 \end{bmatrix};B_{S} = \frac{1}{2}\left( B + B^{T} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & - 1 \end{bmatrix};Q = B_{S} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & - 1 \end{bmatrix}

A matrix of quadratic form is equal to a matrix of corresponding bilinear form.

Classification

Definition: A positive definite form: \mathcal{Q:V}\mathbb{\rightarrow R:\forall}x \in \mathcal{V,}x \neq o:\mathcal{Q}(x) > 0.

Definition: A negative definite form: \mathcal{Q:V}\mathbb{\rightarrow R:\forall}x \in \mathcal{V,}x \neq o:\mathcal{Q}(x) < 0.

Definition: A positive semi definite form: \mathcal{Q:V}\mathbb{\rightarrow R:\forall}x \in \mathcal{V,}x \neq o:\mathcal{Q}(x) \geq 0.

Definition: A negative semi definite form: \mathcal{Q:V}\mathbb{\rightarrow R:\forall}x \in \mathcal{V,}x \neq o:\mathcal{Q}(x) \leq 0.

Definition: A indefinite form: \mathcal{Q:V}\mathbb{\rightarrow R:\exists}x \in \mathcal{V,}x \neq o:\mathcal{Q}(x) < 0 \land \exists y \in \mathcal{V,}y \neq o:\mathcal{Q}(x) > 0.

Example: Classify \mathcal{Q}(x) = - 2x_{1}^{2} + 2x_{1}x_{2} - 3x_{2}^{2}.

\mathcal{Q}(x) = - 2x_{1}^{2} + 2x_{1}x_{2} - 3x_{2}^{2} = - 2\left( x_{1}^{2} - x_{1}x_{2} + \frac{1}{4}x^{2} \right) - \frac{5}{2}x_{2}^{2} = \ - 2\left( x_{1} - \frac{1}{2}x_{2} \right)^{2} - \frac{5}{2}x_{2}^{2}\overset{\begin{aligned} s_{1} & = x_{1} - \frac{1}{2}x_{2} \\ s_{2} & = x_{2} \end{aligned}}{\rightarrow} - 2s_{1}^{2} - \frac{5}{2}s_{2}^{2}, aka it’s negative definite form.

\lbrack Q\rbrack_{N} = \begin{bmatrix} - 2 & 1 \\ 1 & - 3 \end{bmatrix};s = \left( S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & - \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \right)x;\lbrack Q\rbrack_{S} = \begin{bmatrix} - 2 & 0 \\ 0 & - \frac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix}; We diagnolised our matrix.

Congruent operations

Perform a row operation and at the same time the corresponding column operation.

\lbrack Q\rbrack_{N} = \begin{bmatrix} - 2 & 1 \\ 1 & - 3 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} - 2 & 1 \\ 0 & - \frac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} - 2 & 0 \\ 0 & - \frac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix}

For all symmetric matrices \left\lbrack A \middle| I \right\rbrack \sim \lbrack D|\widetilde{L}\rbrack is called LD decomposition.

Example: Classify \mathcal{Q}(x):Q = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & - 1 & 0 \\ - 1 & 2 & - 1 \\ 0 & - 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}

\begin{bmatrix} 2 & - 1 & 0 \\ - 1 & 2 & - 1 \\ 0 & - 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 6 & - 2 \\ 0 & - 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 6 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{4}{3} \end{bmatrix}, it is a positive definite form.

Inner Product

Definition: Inner product on the real vector space \mathcal{V} is a symmetric bilinear form on \mathcal{V}, which corresponding quadratic form is a positive definite one.

\begin{aligned} (u + v,w) & = (u,w) + (v + w) \\ (\alpha u,v) & = \alpha(u,v) \\ (u,v) & = (v,u) \\ (u,u) & > 0;u \neq 0 \end{aligned}

Example: Examine, whether (f,g) = f(0)g(0) + f(1)g(1) is an inner product. (HOWTO: Examine all 4 conditions.)

Example: Examine, whether (x,y)_{A} = x^{T}Ay is an inner product. (It is.)

Norms

Definition: The norm of the vector on the real vector space \mathcal{V} is a positive definite one quadratic form.

\left\| x + y \right\| \leq \left\| x \right\| + \left\| y \right\|\ (triangular inequality)
{\left\| \alpha x \right\| = \alpha\left\| x \right\| }{\left\| x \right\| = 0 \Leftrightarrow x = 0}

Example: \left\| x \right\|_{2} = \sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}} (Euclidian norm); \left\| x \right\|_{0} = \sum_{i}^{n}{|x_{i}|} (zero norm); \left\| x \right\|_{\infty} = \max_{i}{|x_{i}|} (maximum norm)

Example: Energy norms: (x,y)_{A} = x^{T}Ay;\left\| x \right\|_{A} = \sqrt{(x,y)_{A}}

Cauchy–Bunyakovsky–Schwarz inequality: (x,y)^{2} \leq (x,x) \bullet (y,y)

Orthonormality

Definition: An orthonormal means that the norm of all vectors is equal to one and is orthogonal.
Definition: An orthogonal means, that the inner product of any two vector is equal to zero.

Gram-Schmidt Algorithm: Make a set of vectors to orthonormal set.

  1. Take the first vector from the set.

  2. Orthogonalize the vector. We know {f'}_{n} = e_{n} - \alpha_{n - 1}f_{n - 1} - \ldots, where \alpha_{y} = \left( f_{y},e_{n} \right),\ldots.

  3. Orthonormalize the vector – divide it by its norm.

Example: Find an orthonormal basis: (x,y)_{A} = x^{T}Ay;\ A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & - 1 & 0 \\ - 1 & 2 & - 1 \\ 0 & - 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix};\left\| x \right\|_{A} = \sqrt{x^{T}Ax}\mathcal{;V =}\mathbb{R}^{3}

\varepsilon = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\};f = \left\{ \frac{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}}{\left\| \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \right\| = \sqrt{2}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},\frac{\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}}{\left\| \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \right\| = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \bullet \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2},\frac{\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{2}{3} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}}{\left\| \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{2}{3} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\| = \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{2}{3} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \bullet \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \right\}

Example: Find an orthonormal basis of \mathcal{P}^{2}: (p,q) = \int_{0}^{1}{p(x)q(x)dx};\left\| p \right\|_{A} = \sqrt{\int_{0}^{1}{p(x)^{2}dx}}

\varepsilon = \left\{ 1;x;x^{2} \right\};f = \left\{ \frac{x^{2}}{\left\| x^{2} \right\| = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{5}} = \sqrt{5}x^{2},\frac{x - \frac{5}{4}x^{2}}{\left\| x - \frac{5}{4}x^{2} \right\| = \frac{\sqrt{12}}{12}} = - 5\sqrt{3}x^{2} + 4\sqrt{3}x,\ldots \right\}

Variation Principle

Theorem: The matrix A is symmetric and positive definite, (x,y)_{A} = x^{T}Ay, b(x) = b^{t}x, q(x) = \frac{1}{2}(x,x)_{A} - b(x), vectors b and \overline{x}.
A\overline{x} = b \Leftrightarrow (x,y)_{A} = b(y),\forall y \in \mathbb{R}^{n} \Leftrightarrow !\exists\overline{x}:q\left( \overline{x} \right) \leq q(x)

Notes: q(x) is an error function. The solution \overline{x} has the smallest error possible.

Example: A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & - 1 & 0 \\ - 1 & 2 & - 1 \\ 0 & - 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix},b = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},f = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \bullet \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2},\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{2}{3} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \bullet \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \right\}. Solve Ax = b.

Let’s use the middle equation (x,y)_{A} = b(y),y \in f. We can then count only \begin{array}{r} \alpha_{1} = b\left( f_{1} \right) \\ \cdots \\ \alpha_{n} = b\left( f_{n} \right) \end{array}.

Least Square Method

r(x) = Ax - b is called the residual vector. It changes the problem to minimalize the residuum.

Theorem: The minimal residuum is achieved at A^{T}Ax = A^{T}b

Example: \begin{aligned} x_{1} + x_{2} & = 0 \\ 2x_{1} + x_{2} & = 4 \\ 3x_{1} + x_{2} & = 4 \end{aligned}

A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix};A^{T} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix};b = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 4 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix};A^{T}A = \begin{bmatrix} 14 & 6 \\ 6 & 3 \end{bmatrix};A^{T}b = \begin{bmatrix} 20 \\ 8 \end{bmatrix};x = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ - \frac{4}{3} \end{bmatrix}

Spectral Decomposition

Spectral decomposition, a.k.a. diagonalization, is concerned with a linear transformation A_{F,F} = T \bullet A_{E,E} \bullet T^{- 1}, where the matrix A_{E,E} ≔ \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_{2} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda_{n} \end{bmatrix}. Assume, that for some A it holds true, A \bullet e_{i} = \lambda_{i} \bullet e_{i} and e_{1},\ldots,e_{n} are linearly independent. That implies A \bullet (T ≔ \left\lbrack e_{1},\ldots,e_{n} \right\rbrack) = \left\lbrack \lambda_{1} \bullet e_{1},\ldots,\lambda_{n} \bullet e_{n} \right\rbrack, and so A \bullet T = T \bullet \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_{2} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda_{n} \end{bmatrix} .

Conclusion 1: For any matrix A \in C^{n \times n}: A = T \bullet D \bullet T^{- 1}.

Assume that A is real and symmetric. We know A \bullet e = \lambda \bullet e. We can multiple by e^{*} \bullet to get \left( e^{*} \right)^{T} \bullet A \bullet e = \lambda \bullet \left\| e \right\|^{2}. Now we know e^{T} \bullet A^{T} \bullet e^{*} = e^{T} \bullet A \bullet e^{*} = e^{T} \bullet (A \bullet e)^{*} = e^{T} \bullet \lambda^{*} \bullet e^{*} = \lambda^{*} \bullet \left\| e \right\|^{2}, so \lambda\mathbb{\in R}.

Conclusion 2: For any real symmetric matrix A \in C^{n \times n}: \forall\lambda_{i}:\lambda_{i}\mathbb{\in R}

Assume that A is real and symmetric. We know A \bullet e = \lambda \bullet e. We can multiply by e_{j} \bullet, where \lambda_{i} \neq \lambda_{j}. Now e_{j}^{T} \bullet A \bullet e_{i} = \lambda_{i} \bullet e_{j}^{T} \bullet e_{i}, so e_{j}^{T} \bullet A \bullet e_{i} = e_{i}^{T} \bullet A^{T} \bullet e_{j} = e_{i}^{T} \bullet \left( A \bullet e_{j} \right) = \lambda_{j} \bullet e_{i}^{T} \bullet e_{j}. Now because \left( \lambda_{i} - \lambda_{j} \right) \bullet \left( e_{i} \bullet e_{j} \right) = 0.

Conclusion 3: For any real symmetric matrix A \in C^{n \times n}: \forall\left( e_{i},e_{j} \right):e_{i}\bot e_{j},\lambda_{i} \neq \lambda_{j}

A = T \bullet \left( D \in R^{n \times n} \right) \bullet T^{- 1} = Q \bullet D \bullet Q^{T}, where Q is orthogonal matrix

Example: Compute the spectral decomposition of A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}.

|A - \lambda I| = \left| \begin{matrix} 3 - \lambda & 2 \\ 2 & 1 - \lambda \end{matrix} \right| = \lambda^{2} - 4\lambda - 1;\lambda_{1} = 2 + \sqrt{5},\lambda_{2} = 2 - \sqrt{5}

\lambda_{1} = 2 + \sqrt{5}:e_{1}' = t \bullet \begin{bmatrix} - 2 \\ 1 - \sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix},\ t^{2} \bullet \alpha = 1 \Rightarrow e_{1} = \begin{bmatrix} - \frac{2}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \\ \frac{1 - \sqrt{5}}{\sqrt{\alpha}} \end{bmatrix}

\lambda_{2}:e_{2} = s \bullet \begin{bmatrix} - 2 \\ 1 + \sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix},s = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\beta}},\beta = 4 + \left( 1 + \sqrt{5} \right)^{2}

Q = \left\lbrack e_{1},e_{2} \right\rbrack;D = \begin{bmatrix} 2 + \sqrt{5} & 0 \\ 0 & 2 - \sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix};A = Q \bullet D \bullet Q^{T}
